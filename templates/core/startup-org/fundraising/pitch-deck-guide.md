## Purpose

Your pitch deck tells your company's story to investors. It should inspire, inform, and lead to a follow-up conversation. At the pre-seed/seed stage, investors bet on team, market, and early traction - not financials or detailed plans.

**Start-Up Reality:**
- Your deck will evolve for each investor (slight customization)
- First version will be imperfect - that's okay
- Fundraising takes 3-6 months typically
- Expect to iterate the deck 10+ times based on feedback

## Pitch Deck Structure (10-15 Slides)

### Slide 1: Company Vision / Hook
**Purpose:** Grab attention in first 10 seconds

**What to include:**
- Company name and one-liner
- Founder names and email
- A bold, aspirational vision statement

**Example:**
```
[Company Name]
"Making AI accessible to every developer"

Founded by [Name] and [Name]
[email]
```

**Tips:**
- Lead with the dream, not the features
- Make it memorable and repeatable
- Use visuals, not just text

---

### Slide 2: The Problem
**Purpose:** Establish that this is a painful problem worth solving

**What to include:**
- 2-3 specific pain points your customers face
- Why current solutions don't work
- Use customer quotes if you have them

**Example:**
```
Developers want to add AI to their products, but...

‚ùå "Hiring ML engineers takes 6+ months and costs $300K/year"
‚ùå "Existing tools require PhD-level knowledge we don't have"
‚ùå "OpenAI APIs are great for demos but hard to productionize"

‚Üí Result: Companies delay AI features for months or years
```

**Tips:**
- Use actual customer language/quotes
- Make it visceral and relatable
- Don't oversell - be honest about the problem
- Visual: Show frustrated developer, complex diagram, or timeline

---

### Slide 3: The Solution
**Purpose:** Show how elegantly you solve the problem

**What to include:**
- What you do in 2-3 sentences
- How it works (simple diagram or demo screenshot)
- Key benefit/outcome

**Example:**
```
Platform that lets developers add AI to their apps in minutes

[Screenshot of simple API code]

"Add AI features in 5 minutes, not 6 months"
```

**Tips:**
- Show, don't just tell (screenshot or diagram)
- Focus on the outcome, not features
- Keep it simple - details come later
- Visual: Product screenshot or simple architecture diagram

---

### Slide 4: Product Demo / How It Works
**Purpose:** Make it tangible and real

**What to include:**
- Live product screenshots (annotated)
- Before/after comparison
- Simple workflow diagram

**Example:**
```

1. Copy API key ‚Üí  [screenshot]
2. Add 3 lines of code ‚Üí  [code screenshot]
3. Ship to production ‚Üí  [live app screenshot]

```

**Tips:**
- Use actual product screenshots, not mockups
- Show the "aha moment"
- Include specific numbers (time, lines of code, etc.)
- Visual: Annotated screenshots or screen recording stills

- Show convergence of factors

### Slide 6: Market Opportunity
**Purpose:** Show this is a big, growing market worth pursuing

**What to include:**
- TAM (Total Addressable Market)
- Your specific target market (ICP)
- Market growth rate

**Example:**
```

‚Ä¢ 50,000+ venture-backed startups globally
```

**Tips:**
- Be realistic, not pie-in-the-sky TAM
- Show bottoms-up calculation for your ICP
- Explain beachhead and expansion strategy
- Visual: Market sizing pyramid or funnel

### Slide 7: Business Model / How You Make Money
**Purpose:** Show clear path to revenue

**What to include:**
- Pricing model
- Unit economics (if you have them)
- Revenue to date (if any)

**Example:**
```

Average Customer: $2,000/month
Gross Margin: 70%

```

**Tips:**
- Keep it simple and understandable
- Show you've thought about unit economics
- Include early revenue if you have it (even $1K matters)
- Visual: Pricing tiers or customer LTV diagram

## Pitch Deck Best Practices

### Design
- **Clean and simple**: Don't clutter slides
- **Visual-first**: Use images, charts, screenshots - not walls of text
- **Consistent style**: Use template, consistent fonts/colors
- **Readable**: Large fonts (24pt minimum), high contrast
- **Professional but authentic**: Don't over-design

### Content
- **Tell a story**: Problem ‚Üí Solution ‚Üí Why Us ‚Üí Traction ‚Üí Vision
- **Be concise**: 10-15 slides max for initial deck
- **Use data**: Specific numbers, not vague claims
- **Show, don't tell**: Screenshots, customer quotes, growth graphs
- **Be honest**: Don't overhype or hide challenges

### Mistakes to Avoid
- ‚ùå Too many slides (>20 slides for initial pitch)
- ‚ùå Walls of text (slides should be scannable in 5 seconds)
- ‚ùå No traction (even small numbers matter)
- ‚ùå Unclear ask (be specific about raise amount)
- ‚ùå Generic market size ("$1T TAM")
- ‚ùå No differentiation (why not just use [competitor]?)
- ‚ùå Weak team slide (this is critical for seed)
- ‚ùå Ignoring competition ("we have no competitors")

## Different Versions of Your Deck

### 1. Email Deck (Send-Ahead)
- **Purpose:** Get a meeting
- **Format:** PDF, self-explanatory
- **Length:** 12-15 slides
- **Style:** More context, can read standalone
- **Notes:** Include captions, ensure clarity without narration

### 2. Presentation Deck (Live Pitch)
- **Purpose:** Tell story in meeting
- **Format:** Slides with visuals
- **Length:** 10-12 slides (20 min presentation)
- **Style:** Visual-heavy, less text, you narrate
- **Notes:** Leave time for Q&A

### 3. Follow-Up Deck (Post-Meeting)
- **Purpose:** Provide details after initial interest
- **Format:** PDF with appendix
- **Length:** 15-20 slides (includes appendix)
- **Style:** Comprehensive, answers common questions
- **Notes:** Include financials, technical details, customer references

## Customizing for Different Investors

### For Angel Investors
- Emphasize: Team, vision, personal connection to problem
- De-emphasize: Detailed market sizing, exit strategy

### For Seed VCs
- Emphasize: Traction, product-market fit signals, growth potential
- De-emphasize: Long-term vision, detailed operations

### For Strategic Investors
- Emphasize: How you complement their portfolio, partnership potential
- De-emphasize: Generic market opportunity

## Testing Your Pitch Deck

**Before sending to investors, test with:**
- Fellow founders (get honest feedback)
- Advisors (especially those who've raised)
- Friends not in tech (can they understand it?)
- AI assistants (ask them to poke holes in logic)

**Questions to ask yourself:**
- Can someone understand our business in 2 minutes?
- Is the problem clear and painful?
- Is the solution obvious once you see it?
- Do we show traction, however small?
- Is the team credible?
- Would I invest based on this deck?

## Common Investor Questions (Prepare Answers)

**Product:**
- Why can't I just use [competitor]?
- What's your unfair advantage?
- How do you prevent big tech from copying you?

**Market:**
- How big is the market really?
- Who's the ideal customer?
- Why now?

**Traction:**
- What's your retention?
- What's customer acquisition cost?
- How do you know customers love it?

**Team:**
- Why are you the right people to build this?
- What if [competitor] launches this tomorrow?
- What don't you know that worries you?

**Fundraise:**
- How much runway does this give you?
- What milestones will you hit?
- When will you raise next round?
